EU Product Safety and Recall Compliance Interpretation
======================================================

  

**I. Background**
-----------------

In the EU market, consumer health and safety are of paramount importance. The EU has established strict legal frameworks and regulatory mechanisms to protect consumer safety, including product recalls. When a product is recalled, sellers may bear certain responsibilities, including but not limited to: the full cost of the recall, consumer litigation costs, and civil, administrative, or even criminal liabilities resulting from the sale of unsafe or non-compliant products. Sellers should understand the relevant regulations on EU product safety and recall to ensure compliance in the EU market.

  
**II. Compliance Interpretation**
------------------------------------

### **1\. Corrective Measures Related to Product Safety**

**1.1 Overview**  
Manufacturers, distributors, and other operators must take immediate action when they become aware that their products on the market pose a danger. Actions include but are not limited to:  
\- Labeling products with appropriate warnings for specific conditions that pose risks;  
\- Restricting sales conditions to ensure product safety;  
\- Completely banning the sale of certain products;  
\- Destroying products;  
\- Technically repairing/modifying products;  
\- Withdrawing products from the market;  
\- Recalling products.  
**1.2 Types of Recalls**  
A recall involves measures by which operators retrieve dangerous products from consumers or other end-users. Recalls can be conducted voluntarily by operators, usually based on guidance and recommendations from Market Surveillance Authorities (MSA). When the MSA orders operators to initiate a recall, it is considered a mandatory recall. In special cases, when no other feasible measures are available to prevent the involved risks (e.g., when operators cannot be identified or fail to take action), the MSA may need to take corrective measures independently, including recalling products.

###   
**2\. Main EU legal acts relevant to recalls**

**2.1** [**General Product Safety Directive (GPSD, 2001/95/EC)**](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/ALL/?uri=CELEX%3A32001L0095)  
The primary EU-level legislation is the General Product Safety Directive (GPSD) 2001/95/EC. Regarding product recalls, the GPSD outlines the obligations of manufacturers and distributors, the obligations and powers of member states, and the Rapid Information Exchange System (RAPEX) content. These requirements apply to both non-harmonized and harmonized goods (if the latter’s legislation does not already include provisions with the same objectives).  
**2.2** [**Regulation (EU) 2019/1020 on Market Surveillance and Product Compliance**](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex:32019R1020)  
This regulation sets out market surveillance rules for harmonized goods, i.e., products subject to common rules across the EU, such as toys, electrical appliances, or personal protective equipment. It defines the powers of market surveillance authorities, including the power to order product withdrawals or recalls, and the power to remove product listings from online platforms if no other effective measures are available to eliminate serious risks.  
**2.3** [**E-Commerce Directive (2000/31/EC)**](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/ALL/?uri=celex%3A32000L0031)  
This directive establishes the notice and take-down procedures for illegal content, such as dangerous products for sale. Under this directive, hosting service providers can be exempt from liability if they act promptly to remove or disable access to illegal content once they become aware of its nature.  
**2.4** [**General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR, 2016/679)**](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj)  
The GDPR standardizes data protection rules across the EU. Therefore, it must be considered when collecting or processing personal data for recall purposes.  
**2.5** [**Digital Services Act (EU) 2022/2065**](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=celex%3A32022R2065)  
This regulation imposes clear obligations on digital service providers, requiring internet platforms and companies operating in the EU to enhance the review of illegal content and protect user data, timely removal of illegal and harmful online content, and promoting transparency and product liability mechanisms on online platforms.  

### **3\. Roles and Responsibilities of Economic Operators in the Recall Process**

Depending on the situation, any or all operators in the supply chain may be responsible for the recall.  
According to the GPSD, Where a producer or distributor becomes aware that their products on the market pose a danger, they shall immediately notify national competent authorities and provide details of the actions taken to prevent consumer risk. To help operators comply with this obligation, the European Commission has established a specific tool, [the Product Safety Business Alert Gateway](https://webgate.ec.europa.eu/safety-business-gateway/screen/public/home).  
\- Producers have the primary responsibility for the safety of the products they place on the market. If it is necessary to avoid risks to the health and safety of consumers, they have the obligation to take appropriate action (including withdrawing the dangerous product from the supply chain, warning consumers or, as a measure of last resort, recalling products that have already been supplied to consumers).  
\- Distributors must act with due care and must not supply products they know to be unsafe. They must also cooperate with manufacturers and competent authorities in actions taken to avoid risks and pass on information about product risks. This may include cooperating with the issuance of recall notices, directly contacting consumers who purchased the recalled products, and/or cooperating in collecting recalled products and returning them to the manufacturer. In practice, if the manufacturer is not in the country where the recall occurs, the distributor is the primary contact for the MSA.  
Regulation (EU) 2019/1020 contains a requirement to have a dedicated economic operator in the EU who is responsible for each product placed on the EU marke. This can be a manufacturer, importer, authorized representative, or a fulfillment service provider if the others are not established in the EU. Their duties include notifying authorities of dangerous products and cooperating with relevant authorities (including taking corrective measures and mitigating risks).

###   
**4\. Recall Process from the point of view of economic operators**

**4.1 Being ready for a Recall**  
\- Establishing a recall strategy  
\- Establishing an action plan  
\- Appointing a corrective action team  
\- Establish product safety monitoring procedures  
\- Establishing product traceability strategies  
\- Establishing strategies to facilitate direct consumer contact  
\- Identifying legal obligations  
\- Establishing a communication strategy  
\- Identifying possible remedies and ways to retrieve the products  
\- Developing a contingency plan for dealing with retrieved products  
\- Simulating recall events  
**4.2 Performing a Recall**  
1\. Determining the level of risk  
2\. Deciding on which kind of action to take  
3\. Informing the national MSA  
a. Use [the Product Safety Business Alert Gateway](https://webgate.ec.europa.eu/safety-business-gateway/screen/public/home) ([instructions provided](https://webgate.ec.europa.eu/safety/consumers/consumers_safety_gate/home/documents/BusinessGatewayUserManual.pdf))  
b. Contact relevant authorities  
4\. Ensuring a consistent approach to cross-border recalls  
5\. Coordinating and cooperating with other actors  
6\. Communicating recall to consumers  
a. Channels for recall communication:  
i. Direct communication with consumers if possible (letters, emails, phone calls, SMS)  
ii. If direct contact with all consumers is not feasible, use various communication methods (company website, company social media accounts, consumer organization and industry association websites)  
iii. Adapt communication tools to consumer characteristics (e.g., use traditional media like local newspapers and TV broadcasts for older and less digitally-confident audiences)  
iv. Activate word-of-mouth (e.g., Consumers can be given incentives to spread the information about the recall on their social networks or within their own networks)  
v. Explore new communication methods (e.g., leverage influencers on social media to spread recall information)  
vi. Utilize resources of other actors (e.g., seek help from consumer organizations to spread recall information)  
vii. Leverage other actors (e.g., allow consumers to file complaints or get more information via phone or online chat)  
viii. Ensure two-way communication  
b. Content and format of recall notices (official templates provided in Attachment 1):  
i. Format should be prominent and clear  
ii. Use the official language of the local country  
iii. Simple and easy-to-understand wording  
iv. Clear title (e.g., “Product Safety Recall”)  
v. Include product identification information (product name, brand, model, high-resolution images, serial number, batch number, sales period, etc.)  
vi. Clearly state the danger posed by the product and its cause  
vii. Briefly explain the immediate actions to be taken (e.g., stop using the product, how to participate in the recall such as returning the product to the store, arranging for its collection, etc.)  
viii. Provide detailed information about corrective measures (e.g., repair, replacement, or refund)  
ix. Provide contact information to enable consumers to easily get further recall information (e.g., a dedicated toll-free number; other contact forms may include interactive online services or an email address)  
7\. Retrieve products from consumers  
8\. Provide remedies and possible additional incentives to consumers  
9\. Handle retrieved products  
10\. Monitor recall progress  
11\. Adjust or expand recall actions  
12\. End the recall action  
13\. Learn from the experience
